# Objective Types: Flow Patterns and Design Guidance

Each objective type has a natural flow that, when followed, maximizes the likelihood of achieving the desired outcome. These patterns are starting points — adapt based on audience, content complexity, and time constraints.

---

## Information Sharing

**Intent:** Transfer knowledge the audience needs to have — new data, market shifts, organizational updates, capability briefings.

**Audience mode:** Receiving → Processing → Questioning

**Natural flow:**
1. **Context frame** — Why this information matters now (1-2 minutes)
2. **Headline first** — The key takeaway stated upfront so the audience knows where you're going
3. **Supporting detail** — Structured evidence organized by theme, not chronology
4. **Implications** — What this means for the audience's domains, decisions, or priorities
5. **Q&A / discussion** — Let the room process and probe

**Design traps to avoid:**
- Building up to a reveal — executives want the punchline first
- Covering everything — select the 3-5 points that matter most to THIS audience
- Presenting data without interpretation — raw data without "so what" frustrates senior leaders
- No clear "what do you do with this?" — information sharing without implications feels like a waste of time

**Discussion design:** Information sharing meetings often have lower natural discussion energy. Drive engagement by connecting information to the audience's active priorities ("this directly affects the timeline on Project X") or by surfacing a tension the data reveals.

**Success indicators:**
- Audience can restate the key points in their own words
- Follow-up questions are about implications and application, not basic comprehension
- No one leaves saying "what was that meeting about?"

## Progress Update

**Intent:** Report status on initiatives, programs, OKRs, or workstreams. Align the room on what's on track, what's at risk, and what needs intervention.

**Audience mode:** Monitoring → Evaluating → Intervening (if needed)

**Natural flow:**
1. **Overall status headline** — One sentence: where do things stand? (Green/Yellow/Red or equivalent)
2. **What's working** — Brief, high-confidence summary of on-track items (don't dwell here)
3. **What's at risk** — The items that need the audience's attention, with root cause and mitigation underway
4. **What's blocked** — Items requiring the audience's decision, resource allocation, or escalation support
5. **Outlook** — What to expect in the next reporting period, key milestones ahead
6. **Asks** — Specific requests: unblock X, approve Y, reallocate Z

**Design traps to avoid:**
- Exhaustive item-by-item review — executives want exceptions, not the full list
- Burying bad news in the middle — surface risks early and directly
- Status without trajectory — "we're behind" is incomplete without "here's how we recover"
- No asks — if everything is fine, this meeting didn't need to happen; if something isn't, say what you need

**Discussion design:** Focus discussion time on the at-risk and blocked items. Present enough context for the audience to make informed decisions, but don't force them to sit through items that don't need their attention.

**Success indicators:**
- Audience is aligned on the true status (no surprises after the meeting from a different channel)
- Blocked items get unblocked or have a clear path to resolution
- At-risk items have agreed mitigation plans with owners

## Research / Analysis

**Intent:** Present findings from analysis, research, or investigation. Help the audience understand what was found, what it means, and what to do about it.

**Audience mode:** Learning → Evaluating methodology → Drawing conclusions → Deciding on action

**Natural flow:**
1. **Research question** — What were we trying to find out and why?
2. **Bottom line** — The answer, stated upfront (Pyramid Principle)
3. **Methodology summary** — How we arrived at this answer (brief — enough for credibility, not a methods lecture)
4. **Key findings** — 3-5 findings organized by significance, each with supporting evidence
5. **Implications** — What these findings mean for strategy, operations, or decisions ahead
6. **Recommendations** — What we suggest doing based on these findings, with trade-offs
7. **Limitations and open questions** — What we don't know, what further analysis could reveal

**Design traps to avoid:**
- Chronological storytelling ("first we did X, then we found Y") — answer-first, always
- Over-presenting methodology — the audience cares about what you found, not how you found it (unless credibility is in question)
- Findings without recommendations — analysis without a "so what now?" frustrates action-oriented executives
- Presenting certainty when uncertainty exists — flag confidence levels explicitly

**Discussion design:** Research presentations invite methodological challenge ("how confident are you in this?") and implications debate ("what does this mean for our Q4 plan?"). Prepare for both by including a methodology backup appendix and by framing implications as "if-then" statements the room can evaluate.

**Success indicators:**
- Audience trusts the findings and understands the key takeaways
- Recommendations are discussed, challenged, and either adopted or refined
- Further analysis needs are identified and scoped (not left vague)

## Decision(s) Needed

**Intent:** Frame one or more decisions, present options with trade-offs, state a recommendation, and secure a choice from the room.

**Audience mode:** Understanding context → Evaluating options → Debating trade-offs → Committing to a choice

**Natural flow:**
1. **Decision context** — Why this decision is needed now, what happens if we don't decide
2. **Decision criteria** — What factors should drive this choice (align the room on evaluation lens BEFORE presenting options)
3. **Options presented** — 2-4 viable options, each with: description, pros, cons, risks, resource requirements, and timeline implications
4. **Recommendation** — Your recommended option with clear rationale tied to the decision criteria
5. **Discussion** — Structured around the trade-offs, not re-litigating the criteria
6. **Decision capture** — Restate the decision made, any conditions or caveats, and immediate next steps

**Design traps to avoid:**
- Presenting options without a recommendation — executives expect a point of view; presenting a neutral menu signals lack of conviction or analysis
- Too many options (5+) — reduces decision quality; 2-3 is ideal, 4 is the max
- Criteria introduced after options — this allows the room to cherry-pick criteria that support a preferred option; set criteria first
- Not naming trade-offs explicitly — every option has downsides; hiding them erodes trust
- Leaving without a decision — if the room can't decide, capture what's needed to decide and set a deadline

**For multiple decisions in one meeting:**
- Sequence decisions by dependency (if Decision B depends on Decision A's outcome, A goes first)
- Each decision gets its own mini-cycle (context → criteria → options → recommendation → decision)
- Time-box each decision explicitly — compound decision meetings easily run over
- Consider whether decisions can be batched (similar type, same criteria) or must be treated independently

**Discussion design:** Decision meetings have the highest natural discussion energy. Channel it by (1) aligning on criteria before opening option debate, (2) naming the key trade-off explicitly so debate focuses there, and (3) having a clear recommendation that gives the room something to react to rather than starting from a blank slate.

**Success indicators:**
- A decision is made, or a clear path to decision is agreed (who decides, by when, what's needed)
- The decision is documented with rationale (not just the outcome)
- Dissent is surfaced and addressed, not suppressed
- Next steps from the decision are assigned immediately

## Alignment / Endorsement

**Intent:** Get the group on the same page about a direction, strategy, or approach — and secure their explicit commitment to support it.

**Audience mode:** Understanding vision → Evaluating fit → Committing (or raising concerns)

**Natural flow:**
1. **The vision / direction** — What we're proposing and why (Duarte "what could be" arc works well here)
2. **Strategic rationale** — Why this is the right approach, grounded in data, market context, or organizational need
3. **What changes** — Concrete impact on the audience's domains: resources, priorities, timelines, ways of working
4. **What we need from you** — Explicit ask: endorse this direction, commit resources, champion it to your teams
5. **Concerns and conditions** — Open the floor for concerns; capture conditions under which the group will endorse
6. **Commitment capture** — Restate the endorsement and what each person/function has agreed to

**Design traps to avoid:**
- Assuming alignment = passive agreement — endorsement requires active commitment, not just absence of objection
- Presenting a fait accompli — if the decision is already made, say so; don't pretend to seek input you won't use
- Skipping the "what changes for you" section — people endorse what they understand the cost of
- Not capturing commitment explicitly — verbal nods in a meeting evaporate; document who agreed to what

**Discussion design:** Alignment meetings need space for the audience to voice concerns and negotiate conditions. The more senior the audience, the more they'll want to shape the direction, not just approve it. Design the flow so their input has a visible place to land (conditions, modifications, phasing).

**Success indicators:**
- Each person in the room can articulate the direction and their role in supporting it
- Concerns are surfaced and either resolved or captured with a resolution path
- Commitments are specific: "I will [action] by [date]", not "I'm supportive"

## Problem Resolution

**Intent:** Surface a problem, build shared understanding of root causes, evaluate paths forward, and agree on a resolution approach.

**Audience mode:** Understanding the problem → Diagnosing causes → Evaluating responses → Committing to action

**Natural flow:**
1. **Problem statement** — What's happening, what's the impact, how urgent is it (quantify where possible)
2. **Root cause analysis** — What's causing this, structured diagnostically (not a blame narrative)
3. **What we've tried** — Actions already taken and their results (prevents the room from suggesting things you've already done)
4. **Options for resolution** — 2-3 paths forward with trade-offs, resource needs, and timeline
5. **Recommendation** — Preferred path with rationale
6. **Discussion and decision** — Room evaluates options, challenges assumptions, and agrees on approach
7. **Action plan** — Immediate next steps, owners, and check-in schedule

**Design traps to avoid:**
- Presenting the problem without options — executives want solutions, not just problem descriptions
- Blame framing — root cause analysis must be diagnostic, not prosecutorial
- Minimizing severity to avoid alarm — executives can handle bad news; what they can't handle is being surprised later
- Overcomplicating the analysis — focus on the 1-2 root causes that explain 80% of the problem

**Discussion design:** Problem resolution meetings often get emotional or political. Structure the flow so the diagnosis is data-driven (reducing finger-pointing) and the options are evaluated against agreed criteria (reducing opinion-based debate).

**Success indicators:**
- Audience agrees on the root cause and severity
- A resolution approach is selected with clear rationale
- Action items are specific, owned, and time-bound
- A follow-up cadence is established to track resolution

## Hybrid Objectives

When a presentation has multiple objectives, design a single integrated flow with clear narrative transitions between segments.

**Integration principles:**
- **Identify the dominant objective** — this shapes the overall arc and framework choice
- **Sequence by logic, not by objective type** — if context (information sharing) is needed before a decision, present it first; don't artificially separate "the info part" from "the decision part"
- **Use explicit transitions** — when the meeting shifts from one mode to another (e.g., from update to decision), signal it clearly: "That's where we stand. Now I want to shift to a specific decision we need to make about how to proceed."
- **Allocate time by objective weight** — a meeting that's 80% progress update and 20% decision should spend most time on the update but protect time for the decision

**Common hybrid patterns:**
| Pattern | Flow |
|---------|------|
| Update + Decision | Status overview → Focus on the item requiring decision → Decision cycle → Next steps |
| Research + Alignment | Findings → Implications → Proposed direction → Endorsement ask |
| Information + Problem | Context/data → Problem surfaced by the data → Resolution options → Commitment |
| Multiple decisions | Shared context → Decision 1 cycle → Transition → Decision 2 cycle → Consolidated next steps |
