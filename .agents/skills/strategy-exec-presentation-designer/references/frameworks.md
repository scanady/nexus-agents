# Communication Frameworks: Selection and Application

## Framework Selection Guide

Choose the framework that best serves the presentation's dominant objective. When objectives are hybrid, apply the framework to the dominant segment and adapt supporting segments as needed.

| Framework | Best For | Core Mechanic |
|-----------|----------|---------------|
| **SCQA** | Decision meetings, problem resolution | Creates urgency → frames the question → delivers the answer |
| **Pyramid Principle** | Research, information sharing, analysis readouts | Answer first → grouped supporting arguments → evidence |
| **Situation-Progress-Outlook (SPO)** | Progress updates, status reviews | Where we are → what happened → where we're going |
| **Duarte Story Arc** | Alignment, vision, endorsement, change initiatives | What is → what could be → call to action (repeated contrast) |
| **PAOR** | Problem resolution, incident review | Problem → Analysis → Options → Recommendation |

## SCQA (Situation-Complication-Question-Answer)

**Origin:** Barbara Minto / McKinsey  
**Best for:** Presentations where a decision or action is needed and the audience must understand why NOW.

### Structure
1. **Situation** — The stable, agreed-upon context. Facts the audience already knows or accepts. This builds common ground quickly.
2. **Complication** — What changed, went wrong, or is at risk. This creates tension and urgency. The audience should feel "we can't stay here."
3. **Question** — The strategic question that naturally arises from the complication. This focuses the room on what must be answered.
4. **Answer** — Your recommendation, stated clearly. Everything that follows is supporting evidence.

### Application tips
- The Situation should be 1-2 sentences, not a history lesson. Executives know their context.
- The Complication is the engine of the framework — if it doesn't create urgency, the presentation has no energy.
- The Question should be one the audience recognizes as the right question to ask. If they'd frame it differently, you've lost them.
- State the Answer before defending it. Always.

### Adaptation for multiple decisions
Use a single SCQA frame for the overarching meeting context, then a mini-SCQA for each decision:
- Shared S and C for the meeting
- Individual Q and A per decision point

### Example skeleton
> **S:** "We launched the enterprise platform in Q1 and hit 85% of our adoption target."  
> **C:** "However, churn in the mid-market segment has increased 40% since launch, and our largest competitor just announced a free tier targeting our exact customer profile."  
> **Q:** "How do we defend our mid-market position while continuing the enterprise push?"  
> **A:** "We recommend accelerating the self-serve onboarding track and repricing Tier 2 — here's the analysis."

## Pyramid Principle

**Origin:** Barbara Minto  
**Best for:** Any presentation where the primary goal is to transfer understanding — research readouts, analysis presentations, market briefings.

### Structure
1. **Governing thought** — The single most important message, stated first. If the audience remembers only one thing, this is it.
2. **Key supporting arguments** — 3-4 mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive (MECE) groupings that support the governing thought.
3. **Evidence layer** — Data, examples, or analysis supporting each argument group.

### Application tips
- The governing thought is NOT a topic ("Q3 results"). It IS a statement ("Q3 results show we've turned the corner on profitability, driven by three factors").
- Groups must be MECE — no overlap, no gaps. If a finding doesn't fit neatly, the grouping structure is wrong.
- Go only as deep as the audience needs. Executives typically need the governing thought and supporting arguments. The evidence layer is for Q&A or appendix.
- Each argument group should also lead with its own mini-governing thought.

### Vertical vs. horizontal logic
- **Vertical:** Each level answers "why?" or "how?" about the level above
- **Horizontal:** Items at the same level are MECE and logically ordered (time, importance, process)

### Example skeleton
> **Governing thought:** "We should enter the Asian market through a partnership model rather than direct entry."  
> **Argument 1:** "Regulatory complexity makes direct entry prohibitively slow" (evidence: timeline analysis, legal assessment)  
> **Argument 2:** "Local partnerships provide immediate distribution at 60% lower cost" (evidence: partner landscape, financial model)  
> **Argument 3:** "Our competitors' direct-entry failures validate the partnership approach" (evidence: competitor case studies)

## Situation-Progress-Outlook (SPO)

**Origin:** Common in management consulting and program management  
**Best for:** Recurring status meetings, program reviews, steering committees, OKR check-ins.

### Structure
1. **Situation** — Where things stand right now. Overall health, key metrics, headline status.
2. **Progress** — What happened since last update. Focus on exceptions: what moved, what slipped, what changed.
3. **Outlook** — What's coming next. Key milestones, risks on the horizon, resource needs, decisions upcoming.

### Application tips
- SPO is designed for recurring meetings. Assume the audience has prior context — don't re-explain the initiative every time.
- Spend minimal time on items that are on track. The audience's time is best spent on exceptions (at risk, blocked, ahead of plan).
- The Outlook section is where asks live: "We'll need a decision on X by March 15" or "I'm flagging a resource constraint in Q2."
- Use visual status indicators (RAG status, trend arrows) to let the audience scan status quickly.

### Adaptation for large programs
For programs with multiple workstreams, use a two-tier SPO:
- **Tier 1:** Overall program SPO (2-3 minutes)
- **Tier 2:** Workstream-level SPO only for items that are Yellow/Red or need the audience's attention

### Example skeleton
> **Situation:** "Platform migration is Green overall. 3 of 5 workstreams on track, 1 Yellow (data migration), 1 Red (vendor integration)."  
> **Progress:** "Completed user migration for cohort 1 (12K users, no incidents). Data migration delayed 2 weeks due to schema conflicts. Vendor integration blocked pending their API v2 release."  
> **Outlook:** "Cohort 2 migration starts March 20. Data migration recovery plan in place — targeting original deadline with weekend work. Vendor API: need executive escalation if no update by Friday."

## Duarte Story Arc

**Origin:** Nancy Duarte (Resonate)  
**Best for:** Vision presentations, strategy alignment, endorsement asks, change communication — any time you need emotional buy-in, not just intellectual agreement.

### Structure
The Duarte arc creates persuasive tension through repeated contrast between the current state and the future state:

1. **What Is** — The current reality, grounded in facts the audience recognizes
2. **What Could Be** — The future vision, made vivid and compelling
3. **Alternation** — Repeated contrasts between "what is" (pain, limitation, risk) and "what could be" (opportunity, capability, outcome) — each cycle deepening the case
4. **Call to Action** — The specific commitment you need from the audience to move from "what is" to "what could be"

### Application tips
- The arc works because of contrast. Each "what is" moment should make the audience uncomfortable with the status quo; each "what could be" should make them want the future.
- Don't just describe the future — make the audience see themselves in it. "Imagine your team reviewing real-time dashboards instead of waiting 3 weeks for a report."
- The call to action must be specific. Not "support this initiative" — but "approve the $2M investment and assign your top PM by April 1."
- This framework works best when the presenter has genuine conviction. It falls flat if delivered mechanically.

### When NOT to use
- Pure data/analysis presentations (use Pyramid Principle)
- Routine status updates (use SPO)
- When the audience is hostile — persuasive arcs can feel manipulative to a skeptical room. Use SCQA or PAOR instead to ground in logic.

### Example skeleton
> **What Is:** "Today, our customers wait 72 hours for onboarding support. 30% abandon before activation."  
> **What Could Be:** "With self-serve onboarding, customers activate in under 10 minutes. Competitors already offer this."  
> **What Is:** "Our support team spends 60% of their time on tier-1 onboarding tickets that could be automated."  
> **What Could Be:** "Freed from onboarding tickets, support focuses on retention — where every saved customer is worth $45K ARR."  
> **Call to Action:** "I'm asking for approval to reallocate 3 engineers to the self-serve project for Q2. Here's the build plan."

## PAOR (Problem-Analysis-Options-Recommendation)

**Origin:** Common in consulting and operational management  
**Best for:** Problem resolution, incident reviews, risk escalations, lessons-learned presentations.

### Structure
1. **Problem** — Clear statement of what's wrong, quantified impact, and urgency level
2. **Analysis** — Root cause diagnosis, structured and evidence-based (not a blame narrative)
3. **Options** — 2-4 viable resolution paths, each with trade-offs, costs, risks, and timelines
4. **Recommendation** — Your preferred option with rationale tied to evaluation criteria

### Application tips
- Quantify the problem's impact. "Customer satisfaction is down" is weak. "NPS dropped 15 points in Q2, correlated with a 23% increase in support ticket volume" drives action.
- Analysis must be diagnostic, not prosecutorial. "The rollout was rushed" invites defensiveness. "The compressed timeline reduced testing coverage from 85% to 40%, which explains the defect rate" invites problem-solving.
- Options should be genuinely distinct, not variations of the same approach. Include at least one option the audience might not have considered.
- Always include a "do nothing" or "minimum intervention" option with its consequences — this calibrates the cost of inaction.

### Evaluation criteria guidance
Before presenting options, propose 3-5 criteria the room should use to evaluate them:
- **Impact:** How effectively does this resolve the root cause?
- **Speed:** How quickly does this produce results?
- **Cost:** What resources does this require?
- **Risk:** What could go wrong with this approach?
- **Reversibility:** How easy is it to course-correct if this doesn't work?

Getting the room to agree on criteria before seeing options prevents criteria being retrofitted to justify a preferred choice.

### Example skeleton
> **Problem:** "Enterprise deal close rate dropped from 35% to 22% over the last two quarters. This puts $8M in pipeline at risk."  
> **Analysis:** "Root cause analysis points to two factors: (1) new competitors entering with lower price points, and (2) our demo-to-proposal cycle averaging 18 days vs. industry norm of 7."  
> **Options:** "Option A: Competitive pricing adjustment (fast, high margin impact). Option B: Accelerate demo-to-proposal cycle (addresses root cause, 6-week build). Option C: Both, phased."  
> **Recommendation:** "Option C — implement pricing adjustment immediately to stop bleeding, then accelerate the cycle to address the structural issue."
