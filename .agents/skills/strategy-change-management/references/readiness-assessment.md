# Change Readiness Assessment Framework

A structured framework for evaluating an organization's readiness to absorb and sustain a change initiative.

---

## Assessment Dimensions

### 1. Leadership Alignment

**What to assess:** Whether the leadership team is unified on the need, direction, urgency, and resource commitment for this change.

| Score | Definition | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **High** | Leadership is unified, vocal, and actively sponsoring | Consistent messaging across all leaders; sponsor is visible and engaged; budget and resources are committed; leadership publicly champions the change |
| **Medium** | Leadership is generally supportive but not fully aligned | Some leaders are silent or passive; sponsor is supportive but not highly visible; resource commitment is conditional; some competing priorities |
| **Low** | Leadership is divided, disengaged, or opposing | Conflicting messages from different leaders; no visible sponsor; resources not secured; active opposition or indifference at senior levels |

**Key assessment questions:**
- Can the sponsor articulate the case for change in their own words (not reading a script)?
- Have all senior leaders been briefed and given the opportunity to raise concerns?
- Is there a leadership coalition (not just one sponsor) actively supporting this change?
- Are there known "silent objectors" at the leadership level who could undermine the change?
- Has leadership committed resources (budget, people, time) or are they waiting to see results first?

**Red flags:**
- Sponsor delegates all visible activities to a project manager
- Leadership sends conflicting signals about priority vs. other initiatives
- The change was announced without leadership team discussion
- Leaders say the right things in meetings but don't follow through with actions

---

### 2. Cultural Receptivity

**What to assess:** Whether the organizational culture supports change adoption or creates structural resistance.

| Score | Definition | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **High** | Culture embraces change and continuous improvement | History of successful changes; innovation is valued; experimentation is safe; people adapt quickly |
| **Medium** | Culture is cautious but not hostile to change | Mixed change history; risk-averse but open to evidence; "show me" attitude; needs proof before commitment |
| **Low** | Culture actively resists change | History of failed changes or broken promises; blame culture; "this too shall pass" cynicism; strong attachment to "the way we've always done it" |

**Key assessment questions:**
- What were the last 3 significant changes? How were they received?
- Is there visible change fatigue from recent or concurrent initiatives?
- Do people feel psychologically safe to voice concerns and ask questions?
- Is failure treated as learning or blame?
- Are there strong cultural narratives that anchor identity to current ways of working?

**Red flags:**
- People reference past failed changes in everyday conversation
- "We've seen this before" cynicism is widespread
- Informal networks actively resist or undermine official initiatives
- High turnover in change-adjacent roles (project managers, transformation leads)

---

### 3. Organizational Capacity

**What to assess:** Whether the organization can absorb this change given current workload, competing priorities, and change saturation.

| Score | Definition | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **High** | Organization has bandwidth and energy for this change | Few competing change initiatives; workload is manageable; people have time for training and transition; dedicated change resources available |
| **Medium** | Organization is busy but can accommodate with planning | Some competing initiatives but manageable; people are stretched but not burned out; change will require trade-offs; partial dedicated resources |
| **Low** | Organization is saturated or overwhelmed | Multiple concurrent changes; people are overwhelmed; no bandwidth for training; "just pile it on" sentiment; no dedicated resources |

**Change Saturation Assessment:**
Map all active and planned changes on a timeline. For each, note:
- Impacted groups (overlap with this change?)
- Resource demands (people, budget, attention)
- Timeline conflicts (peak periods stacking?)
- Cumulative emotional toll

**Capacity calculation heuristic:**
- 1-2 concurrent changes impacting same population = manageable with coordination
- 3-4 concurrent changes = risk zone — requires explicit prioritization and sequencing
- 5+ concurrent changes = saturation — do not proceed without deprioritizing something else

**Red flags:**
- People say "I don't have time" before the change is even explained
- Training sessions have low attendance because people can't step away from work
- Last change initiative ran over timeline because the organization couldn't absorb it
- Middle managers are visibly overwhelmed with current demands

---

### 4. Stakeholder Readiness

**What to assess:** Whether the impacted groups are aware, willing, and able to make the transition.

| Score | Definition | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **High** | Stakeholders are aware, largely supportive, and have foundational skills | People understand why the change is needed; skill gaps are small and trainable; early enthusiasm or at least openness; champions are emerging organically |
| **Medium** | Stakeholders are partially aware with mixed reactions | Awareness is inconsistent; some skill gaps requiring significant training; mix of supportive and skeptical attitudes; need for targeted engagement |
| **Low** | Stakeholders are unaware, resistant, or significantly unprepared | Little to no awareness of the change; major skill gaps; strong emotional resistance; no organic champions; union/works council concerns |

**ADKAR-based individual readiness assessment:**
For each major stakeholder group, assess:
- **Awareness** — Do they know the change is happening and why?
- **Desire** — Do they want to participate and support the change?
- **Knowledge** — Do they know how to change (new skills, behaviors, processes)?
- **Ability** — Can they implement the change in their daily work?
- **Reinforcement** — Are there mechanisms to sustain the change?

The weakest ADKAR element for each group is the barrier to focus on first.

**Red flags:**
- Impacted groups learned about the change through rumors, not official communication
- Key stakeholders were not consulted during the design phase
- Required skill gaps are so large that training alone won't close them
- Union or works council has not been consulted where required

---

### 5. Infrastructure Readiness

**What to assess:** Whether the systems, tools, facilities, and resources needed for the change are in place or on track.

| Score | Definition | Indicators |
|-------|-----------|------------|
| **High** | Infrastructure is in place or on track with no critical dependencies | Systems are configured and tested; training materials exist; support structures are staffed; budget is allocated and accessible |
| **Medium** | Infrastructure is partially ready with manageable gaps | Key systems are in progress but not complete; some training materials need development; support structure is planned but not fully staffed; budget is approved but not yet released |
| **Low** | Infrastructure has critical gaps or dependencies | Core systems are not ready; no training infrastructure; support model undefined; budget is not secured; critical vendor/partner dependencies unresolved |

**Infrastructure checklist:**
- [ ] Technology systems configured, tested, and accessible to end users
- [ ] Integrations with existing systems validated
- [ ] Training materials developed and reviewed
- [ ] Training delivery infrastructure available (LMS, classrooms, virtual platforms)
- [ ] Support model defined (helpdesk, super-users, escalation paths)
- [ ] Communication channels confirmed and accessible
- [ ] Data migration (if applicable) planned and tested
- [ ] Rollback/contingency plan documented
- [ ] Security and compliance reviews completed
- [ ] Budget allocated and accessible for execution

**Red flags:**
- Go-live date is set but systems are still in development
- Training materials are being created the week of training delivery
- No defined support model for Day 1 questions
- Critical third-party dependencies have no confirmed delivery dates

## Scoring Summary Template

| Dimension | Score | Key Evidence | Top Risk | Recommended Action |
|-----------|-------|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| Leadership Alignment | H / M / L | [Evidence] | [Risk] | [Action] |
| Cultural Receptivity | H / M / L | [Evidence] | [Risk] | [Action] |
| Organizational Capacity | H / M / L | [Evidence] | [Risk] | [Action] |
| Stakeholder Readiness | H / M / L | [Evidence] | [Risk] | [Action] |
| Infrastructure Readiness | H / M / L | [Evidence] | [Risk] | [Action] |

**Overall Readiness Rating:**
- **5 Highs or 4 Highs + 1 Medium** = Green — proceed with standard plan
- **3+ Mediums** = Amber — proceed with enhanced mitigation for Medium areas
- **Any Low** = Red — do not proceed until Low is addressed or formally risk-accepted by sponsor

---

## Using the Assessment

1. **Assess before planning.** The readiness assessment should inform the change plan — not validate it after the fact.
2. **Be honest.** Inflating readiness scores to get approval creates downstream failures.
3. **Involve stakeholders.** The assessment shouldn't be done in a room by the change team alone. Validate findings with people from the impacted groups.
4. **Reassess at milestones.** Readiness is not static. Reassess at key milestones, especially before go-live.
5. **Use Lows as leverage.** A Low rating is a powerful conversation starter with leadership about what needs to change before the change can succeed.
