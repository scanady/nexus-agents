# Stakeholder Mapping

Advanced stakeholder analysis techniques, engagement matrices, and coalition-building strategies for change management.

---

## Stakeholder Identification

### Identification Methods

**Ripple mapping:** Start from the change epicenter and map outward:
1. **Direct impact** — People whose daily work changes (new tools, new processes, new roles)
2. **Indirect impact** — People whose work is affected downstream (reports change, inputs change, dependencies shift)
3. **Enablers** — People who must act for the change to work (IT, HR, Finance, Legal)
4. **Influencers** — People who shape opinions regardless of formal role (senior ICs, long-tenured staff, union reps)
5. **External** — Customers, partners, vendors, regulators affected by or aware of the change

**Common stakeholder blind spots:**
- Middle managers (the most critical and most overlooked group in change)
- IT operations and support teams (they absorb the day-1 impact)
- Customers or external users who interact with changed processes
- Shared services teams (HR, Finance) who must update their own processes
- New hires who join during the transition and receive no context

### Engagement Strategies by Quadrant

**Partner Closely (High Influence, High Impact)**
- Involve in design decisions and pilot groups
- Give early access to information before broader announcement
- Invite to steering committee or advisory group
- Meet weekly during active change
- Make them co-owners of the change, not just recipients
- Listen to their concerns and visibly act on feedback

**Keep Informed & Aligned (High Influence, Low Impact)**
- Brief regularly but don't over-involve in details
- Ask for their visible endorsement and advocacy
- Address their concerns quickly — they can amplify or undermine messaging
- Leverage their credibility with other groups
- Monthly touchpoints; escalate to weekly if sentiment shifts

**Support Heavily (Low Influence, High Impact)**
- Prioritize training, job aids, and hands-on support
- Provide clear, detailed communication about what changes for them
- Create safe spaces to voice concerns without judgment
- Assign dedicated change champions within their group
- Monitor adoption closely and intervene quickly on barriers

**Monitor (Low Influence, Low Impact)**
- Include in broad communication channels
- Provide self-service information (FAQ, intranet hub)
- Watch for emerging concerns that could escalate
- Periodic check-ins, not dedicated engagement

## Advanced Mapping: The Commitment Curve

For critical stakeholder groups, map where they are today vs. where they need to be:

| Level | Definition | Behavioral Indicators |
|-------|-----------|----------------------|
| **Committed** | Actively championing and driving the change | Volunteering for pilot, advocating to peers, contributing ideas, adjusting own behavior |
| **Supportive** | Will adopt and comply, may encourage others | Attending training willingly, asking constructive questions, adapting workflows |
| **Neutral** | Not resisting, not helping — waiting to see | Quiet in meetings, "tell me what to do", compliant but not enthusiastic |
| **Skeptical** | Has concerns, openly questioning, but not blocking | Raising objections in meetings, asking pointed "what if" questions, expressing doubt |
| **Resistant** | Actively working against the change | Refusing to attend training, openly criticizing, influencing others to resist, reverting to old ways |

**The gap between current and target commitment = your stakeholder engagement workload.**

- Moving Resistant → Neutral requires 3-5x more effort than moving Neutral → Supportive
- Not every stakeholder needs to be Committed — Supportive is sufficient for most groups
- Focus Committed-level engagement on people with high influence who can cascade to others

## Coalition Building

### The Change Coalition Model

Every successful change requires three tiers of support:

**Tier 1: Executive Coalition (3-7 people)**
- The sponsor + senior leaders from impacted areas
- Provides authority, resources, and visible championship
- Must speak about the change consistently and publicly
- Meets biweekly to review progress and resolve escalations

**Tier 2: Manager Network (all people managers of impacted groups)**
- The most critical layer — managers translate strategy into daily reality
- Must be briefed BEFORE their teams, with talking points and authority to answer questions
- Need dedicated support: cascade decks, FAQ, 1:1 coaching from change team
- Common failure: managers are told about the change at the same time as their teams

**Tier 3: Change Champions / Super-Users (1 per 25-50 impacted people)**
- Volunteer advocates selected for credibility, enthusiasm, and proximity to impacted groups
- Provide peer-level support, early feedback, and behavioral modeling
- Need dedicated training, regular briefings, and recognition
- Act as the eyes and ears of the change team within the organization

### Champion Selection Criteria

Select change champions based on:
- **Credibility** — peers respect and listen to them (not necessarily the most senior)
- **Connectivity** — they know people across the organization, not just their immediate team
- **Openness** — willing to try new things and comfortable with ambiguity
- **Communication skill** — can explain complex changes in simple, relatable terms
- **Willingness** — volunteered or enthusiastically agreed (never conscripted)

### Champion Program Design

| Element | Details |
|---------|---------|
| **Briefing cadence** | Weekly during active change, biweekly during sustainment |
| **Briefing content** | What's happening this week, what questions to expect, what feedback to collect |
| **Support provided** | Early training access, dedicated Slack/Teams channel, direct line to change team |
| **Time commitment** | 2-4 hours/week during active change (must be approved by their manager) |
| **Recognition** | Public acknowledgment, certificate of participation, career development credit |
| **Feedback loop** | Champions report back what they hear — this is a two-way channel, not just broadcast |

## Stakeholder Communication Preferences

Map how each group prefers to receive information. Using the wrong channel renders the best message invisible:

| Channel | Best For | Limitations |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Town hall / All-hands** | Big announcements, vision-setting, Q&A with leadership | One-directional, hard to have nuanced conversation, can feel performative |
| **Manager 1:1 cascade** | Personal impact discussions, concerns, individual questions | Depends entirely on manager skill and willingness |
| **Email** | Detailed information, reference material, documentation | Low engagement for emotional or complex topics; easily ignored |
| **Slack/Teams** | Quick updates, peer discussion, informal Q&A | Can feel noisy; important messages get lost in scroll |
| **Intranet/Wiki** | Self-service reference, FAQ, detailed guides | Only works if people know it exists and visit it |
| **Video message** | Humanizing leadership, explaining complex rationale | Can feel inauthentic if over-produced; not interactive |
| **Workshop / Interactive session** | Exploring concerns, co-designing solutions, building buy-in | Time-intensive; requires skilled facilitation |
| **Digital signage / Physical posters** | Ambient awareness, reinforcement of simple messages | No depth; only works for high-traffic physical spaces |

**Rule of thumb:** Use at least 3 channels for any critical message. People need to encounter the same message in different contexts before it registers.

## Managing Political Dynamics

Change always intersects with organizational politics. Acknowledge and navigate, don't pretend it doesn't exist:

**Common political dynamics in change:**
- **Territory protection** — Leaders resist changes that reduce their headcount, budget, or scope
- **Credit competition** — Multiple leaders want to "own" the change for visibility
- **Information hoarding** — People withhold information to maintain power
- **Passive resistance** — Verbal agreement with no follow-through ("pocket veto")
- **Proxy wars** — Opposition surfaces through indirect channels (concerns about "timing" or "resources" when the real issue is disagreement with the direction)

**Navigation strategies:**
- Map the political landscape early — who benefits, who loses, who's neutral
- Give potential losers a visible, meaningful role in the new state
- Create formal decision-making forums so decisions don't happen in hallways
- Document agreements and commitments publicly
- Address pocket vetoes directly: "We agreed on X in the last meeting. I notice Y hasn't happened. What's blocking?"
- When facing proxy resistance, address the proxy concern seriously AND the underlying issue privately
