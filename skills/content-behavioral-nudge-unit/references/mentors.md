# Foundational Scholars

These scholars' work forms the theoretical basis of the Nudge Unit skill. Every nudge must be traceable to concepts from this body of research.

---

## Herbert Simon

American economist and cognitive psychologist. Nobel laureate in Economics (1978).

**Key contributions:**
- **Bounded rationality**: People do not optimise — they *satisfice* (choose the first option that meets a minimum threshold) because cognitive resources, time, and information are limited.
- **Satisficing vs. maximising**: Decision-makers use simplified mental models rather than exhaustive analysis.

**How it informs this skill:**
- Bounded rationality is *why nudges work*. If people were perfectly rational, choice architecture wouldn't matter — they'd always find the optimal option regardless of presentation. Because cognition is bounded, the structure of the decision environment (defaults, framing, simplification) has outsized influence on outcomes.
- When diagnosing behavioural barriers (Phase 1), apply the bounded rationality lens: assume the target population has limited attention, limited information, and limited processing capacity. Design nudges that work *within* these constraints rather than requiring people to overcome them.

---

## Daniel Kahneman

Israeli-American psychologist. Nobel laureate in Economics (2002).

**Key contributions:**
- **Dual-process theory (System 1 / System 2)**: Two distinct cognitive systems — System 1 (fast, automatic, heuristic-driven) and System 2 (slow, deliberate, rule-based) — govern all decision-making.
- **Prospect theory** (with Tversky): People evaluate outcomes relative to a reference point, feel losses ~2× more than equivalent gains (loss aversion), and overweight small probabilities.
- **Cognitive biases**: Anchoring, availability heuristic, representativeness heuristic, framing effects, and many others — systematic, predictable deviations from rational choice.

**How it informs this skill:**
- The System 1 / System 2 model is the **primary categorisation structure** for all nudges. System 1 nudges reshape the environment so the desired behaviour happens automatically (defaults, framing, salience, priming). System 2 nudges provide tools that help people reason better when they're already engaged (calculators, commitment devices, planning prompts, feedback).
- **Prospect theory** provides the core mechanisms for many nudges: loss framing (frame outcomes as losses to avoid rather than gains to achieve), reference-point manipulation (anchoring), and probability weighting (lottery-style incentives attract disproportionate attention).
- When identifying behavioural barriers (Phase 1), apply Kahneman's bias taxonomy: Which biases are blocking the desired behaviour? Which can be leveraged to promote it?

## Amos Tversky

Israeli cognitive psychologist. Worked primarily with Kahneman.

**Key contributions:**
- **Judgment under uncertainty**: Co-identified the heuristics (anchoring, availability, representativeness) people use to make probability estimates — and the systematic biases these heuristics produce.
- **Prospect theory** (with Kahneman): Formalised how people actually evaluate risky choices, replacing expected utility theory with a model that accounts for reference dependence, loss aversion, and diminishing sensitivity.
- **Framing effects**: Demonstrated that logically equivalent options produce different choices depending on how they're presented (e.g., "90% survival rate" vs. "10% mortality rate").

**How it informs this skill:**
- Tversky's bias research is the **empirical backbone** of nudge design. The specific biases he identified (anchoring, availability, representativeness) are the named mechanisms that nudges leverage or counteract.
- **Framing effects** are among the most powerful and most commonly used nudge tools — same information, different frame, different behaviour. Every nudge that involves messaging or communication should consider Tversky's framing research.
- When generating candidate nudges (Phase 2), systematically walk through Tversky's biases as an idea generator: Could we use anchoring? Availability? Framing? Loss aversion?

## Richard Thaler

American economist. Nobel laureate in Economics (2017).

**Key contributions:**
- **Nudge theory** (with Sunstein): Defined a nudge as any aspect of choice architecture that alters people's behaviour in a predictable way without forbidding any options or significantly changing economic incentives.
- **Libertarian paternalism**: The ethical framework for nudging — it is legitimate to influence behaviour for the person's own benefit, *provided* freedom of choice is fully preserved.
- **Mental accounting**: People categorise money and resources into separate mental "accounts" (e.g., spending money vs. savings), which affects how they value and allocate resources.
- **Save More Tomorrow (SMarT)**: Landmark nudge program that increased retirement savings by asking employees to commit *future* raises to savings — exploiting present bias and status quo bias simultaneously.

**How it informs this skill:**
- Thaler's definition of a nudge is the **filter criterion** applied in Phase 3. Every candidate intervention must pass the "nudge test": Does it preserve choice? Is it minimal? Is it cheap? Does it avoid significant economic incentive changes?
- **Libertarian paternalism** sets the ethical boundary. The skill designs interventions that steer choices toward welfare-improving outcomes while keeping all options fully available and opt-out genuinely easy.
- **Mental accounting** is a directly usable nudge mechanism — for example, reframing a cost as a daily amount ("pennies a day") exploits how people mentally categorise small vs. large expenditures.

## Cass Sunstein

American legal scholar. Co-author of *Nudge* (2008) with Thaler. Former Administrator of the White House Office of Information and Regulatory Affairs (OIRA).

**Key contributions:**
- **Choice architecture**: The systematic design of decision environments — how options are presented, which is the default, what information is salient, what the order is, and how complexity is managed — all shape outcomes without restricting options.
- **Regulatory nudges**: Applied choice architecture at government scale — simplified forms, smart defaults, mandatory disclosure redesign, automatic enrolment in public programs.
- **Sludge**: Identified the inverse of a nudge — excessive friction (unnecessary paperwork, confusing processes, hidden options) that discourages people from making choices in their own interest. Sludge audits remove barriers rather than adding nudges.

**How it informs this skill:**
- **Choice architecture** is the operating model of this entire skill. Every nudge is fundamentally a change to the choice architecture — the structure of the decision environment. Phase 0 (Research) and Phase 1 (Diagnose) map the current choice architecture before any intervention is designed.
- **Sludge** is a critical concept for Phase 0's friction audit. Before adding nudges, look for sludge to remove — unnecessary steps, confusing forms, hidden defaults, or unclear information. Removing sludge is often more effective than adding new nudges.
- Sunstein's regulatory work demonstrates that nudges scale: the same principles that work for an individual product sign-up flow work for national policy.
