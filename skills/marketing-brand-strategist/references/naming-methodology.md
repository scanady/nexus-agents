## 1. Brand Strategy Framework

Before generating any names, understand the positioning:

### Discovery Questions
- What does the company/product do? (in plain language)
- Who is the target audience?
- What is the core differentiator?
- What feeling or association should the name evoke?
- What words or themes resonate with the founder/team?
- Are there existing brand elements to preserve or extend?
- What is the scope today vs. the aspiration for tomorrow?

### Positioning Statement Template
"[Brand] is [what it does] for [who], differentiated by [unique angle]. Today it focuses on [current scope] but needs room to grow into [future scope]."

## 2. Name Generation Approaches

Use these six strategies in order of brand strength. The best names come from the top of this list.

### Strategy 1: Metaphor and Analogy
The strongest brand names evoke something without literally describing it. Think Apple (simplicity, knowledge), Amazon (vast, everything), Patagonia (rugged, natural).

Ask: What is a metaphor for what this company does? What transformation occurs? What does the customer become?

Examples of the thinking process:
- A fermentation site transforms raw ingredients into something new = alchemy, transmutation, opus
- An AI company amplifies human capability = telescope, lever, catalyst

### Strategy 2: Domain-Specific Language
Every field has insider vocabulary that outsiders find intriguing. These words signal credibility to the audience while sounding distinctive to everyone else.

Ask: What are the technical terms, tools, processes, or materials in this field? Which ones sound good spoken aloud?

Examples:
- Brewing: wort, krausen, vorlauf, decoction, gravity, attenuation, pellicle
- AI/tech: kernel, mesh, signal, node, graph, cipher

### Strategy 3: Borrowed Words
Words from other languages (especially Latin, Greek, German, Japanese) that relate to the concept but sound fresh in English.

Ask: What is the concept in Latin? Greek? German? Japanese? Is there an archaic English word that fits?

Examples:
- Athanor (alchemist's furnace), Materia (raw material), Opus (great work)
- Kultur (German for culture), Haus (German for house), Werk (German for works)

### Strategy 4: Portmanteau / Fusion
Combine two relevant words into one new word. The best portmanteaus are immediately parseable (you can hear both source words).

Rules for good portmanteaus:
- The blended word should be pronounceable on first read
- Both source words should be recognizable
- Keep it under 10 characters if possible
- The blend point should feel natural (shared sounds help)

Examples: alchembrew (alchemy + brew), brewcodex (brew + codex)

### Strategy 5: Modified Spelling
Take a real word and alter the spelling to make it ownable. Use sparingly and only when the original word is clearly recognizable.

Rules:
- Change no more than 1-2 characters
- The pronunciation should remain obvious
- Avoid substitutions that look like typos (e.g., "kool" for "cool")
- Good substitutions: y for i, k for c, x for cks, dropping a trailing e

Examples: thryve (thrive), augmynt (augment), elevynt (elevate + suffix)

### Strategy 6: Abstract / Invented Words
Completely made-up words. These are the hardest to make memorable but offer the most trademark protection and domain availability.

Rules:
- Must be pronounceable on first read (test: can you say it to someone on the phone and they spell it right?)
- Should feel like it belongs to a language (follow phonotactic rules)
- 5-8 characters is the sweet spot
- Should have pleasant mouth-feel (mix of hard and soft sounds)
- Avoid consonant clusters that are hard to say (e.g., "thryvx")

## 3. Domain Name Strategy

### TLD Priority
1. .com is king for credibility and memorability
2. .io works well for tech companies
3. .ai works for AI-focused companies
4. .co is acceptable but often confused with .com
5. Avoid .net, .org for commercial brands unless the name is exceptional

### Domain-Integrated Naming
For .io and .ai domains, consider names where the TLD completes the word:
- amplif.io, cogit.io, potenti.ai
- This only works if the full word (with TLD) is immediately readable

### Domain Availability Reality
- Short (4-5 letter) .com domains are almost entirely taken or premium
- 6-8 letter .com domains have better availability if the word is truly invented
- Two-word .com domains (brewcodex, culturejar) have surprisingly good availability
- Always check before presenting names to the user

### Checking Domains
- Check in batches of 15-20 domains at a time
- Always check the preferred TLD (.com) first
- For promising names, check .com, .io, and .ai together
- Discard any domain that is premium-priced, aftermarket, or already registered
- Only present domains available at standard registration price

## 4. Evaluation Criteria

Rate every name candidate against these criteria before presenting:

### The Phone Test
Can you say the name to someone on the phone and they spell it correctly on the first try? If not, the name has a friction problem.

### The Memory Test
If someone hears the name once, can they recall it an hour later? Short, rhythmic, and emotionally resonant names pass this test.

### The Relevance Test
Does the name hint at what the company does, or at least not contradict it? Abstract names need more marketing spend to build association.

### The Growth Test
Will this name still fit in 3-5 years as the company expands? Names that are too specific (e.g., "BeerDataLab") box you in.

### The Uniqueness Test
Search for the name. Is there an existing company, product, or trademark with this name or a confusingly similar one? Even if the domain is available, a name collision is a problem.

### The Aesthetic Test
Does the name look good in a logo? Is it visually balanced? Does it have good "mouth feel" when spoken? Names with a mix of hard and soft consonants tend to sound more dynamic.

## 5. Anti-Patterns to Avoid

### Overengineered Portmanteaus
Names like "cognivelocimatrix" that try to cram three concepts into one word. If you can't parse it in under 2 seconds, start over.

### Gratuitous Letter Substitution
"Krazee Brewz" style names that substitute multiple letters. One substitution is a brand choice. Three is a gimmick.

### Generic + Suffix Patterns
Appending -ly, -ify, -io, -zy to generic words. These feel dated and interchangeable (Bookly, Taskify, Shopzy).

### Acronyms as Names
Unless you are IBM or NASA, don't launch with an acronym. They have zero emotional resonance and are impossible to remember.

### Names That Require Explanation
If you need a paragraph to explain why the name is clever, it's not working. The best names feel inevitable after you hear them.

### Too Many Syllables
Four syllables is the practical limit for a brand name. Three is ideal. Beyond four, people will shorten it themselves (and you lose control of the shortening).
