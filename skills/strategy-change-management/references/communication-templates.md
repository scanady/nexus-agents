# Communication Templates

Message templates for each phase of change communication, channel selection guidance, and cascade briefing structures.

---

## Communication Architecture

### The Three-Phase Model

Every change communication plan operates across three phases. Each phase has a different purpose, tone, and cadence:

| Phase | Purpose | Tone | Cadence |
|-------|---------|------|---------|
| **Awareness** | Build understanding of WHY change is needed | Urgent but confident; factual; empathetic | 2-4 communications in the first 2 weeks |
| **Understanding** | Help people grasp WHAT changes and HOW it affects them | Detailed and practical; supportive; role-specific | Weekly during transition |
| **Reinforcement** | Sustain new behaviors and celebrate progress | Celebratory; evidence-based; forward-looking | Biweekly tapering to monthly |

### Communication Principles

1. **Tell managers first.** They must hear news before their teams — always. Brief them 24-48 hours before any broad announcement.
2. **Match the channel to the message weight.** Layoffs by email is unacceptable. A minor process update doesn't need a town hall.
3. **Repeat, repeat, repeat.** People need to encounter the same core message 5-7 times through different channels before it sticks.
4. **Open feedback channels.** Every communication must include a mechanism for questions and concerns — not just a reply-to email.
5. **Use credible senders.** The CEO for strategic vision. The department VP for team-specific impact. The peer champion for how-it-really-works truth.
6. **Acknowledge what's hard.** "We know this is disruptive" is 10x more effective than pretending the change is easy.

## Phase 1: Awareness Templates

### Template A: Executive Announcement (All-Hands or Email)

**Use when:** Announcing a significant change to the entire organization for the first time.
**Sender:** CEO or most senior executive sponsor.
**Channel:** Town hall (preferred) or organization-wide email.

```
Subject: [Concrete subject — not "Exciting Changes Ahead"]

Team,

[1 sentence: What is changing]

[2-3 sentences: Why this change is necessary — business data, market reality, customer feedback, 
or strategic imperative. Be specific. "Because it's the right thing to do" is not a reason.]

[1-2 sentences: What the desired future state looks like — paint a picture people can see themselves in]

[1-2 sentences: Acknowledge the disruption honestly — "I know this creates uncertainty" or 
"This is a significant shift from how we've worked"]

Here's what you can expect:
- [Timeline: When key milestones will happen]
- [Support: What resources will be available — training, Q&A sessions, etc.]
- [Communication: How updates will be shared going forward]

[1 sentence: Where to ask questions or share concerns — specific channel, not "reach out to your manager"]

[Name]
```

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't use "exciting" to describe changes that eliminate jobs or increase workloads
- Don't be vague about timelines ("sometime in Q3")
- Don't skip the "why" and jump to "here's what's happening"
- Don't end with "Thank you for your flexibility" — it's dismissive

### Template B: Manager Cascade Briefing

**Use when:** Briefing managers 24-48 hours before the all-hands announcement so they can prepare for team questions.
**Sender:** Change lead or HR business partner.
**Channel:** Live briefing (virtual or in-person) + written summary with talking points.

```
## Manager Pre-Brief: [Change Name]

### Timing
- This pre-brief: [Date]
- Organization announcement: [Date] — Do not share this information before that date
- Team conversation: [Date range] — You should talk to your team within [timeframe] after the announcement

### What's Changing
[2-3 sentences: Clear, factual description of the change]

### Why Now
[Bullet points: specific business drivers]

### Impact on Your Team
[Specific description of what changes for their team's daily work]

### Your Role as a Manager
- Have a 1:1 or team conversation within [timeframe] after the announcement
- Use the talking points below — adjust to your style, but cover these key messages
- Listen first. Your team needs to feel heard before they'll hear you.
- It's OK to say "I don't know yet, but I'll find out" — better than guessing or deflecting
- Report back any concerns or questions you can't answer to [change team contact]

### Talking Points for Your Team
1. [Message 1 — the what]
2. [Message 2 — the why / business case]
3. [Message 3 — what this means for your team specifically]
4. [Message 4 — what support is available]
5. [Message 5 — timeline and next steps]

### FAQ — Anticipated Questions

**Q: [Common question 1]**
A: [Answer]

**Q: [Common question 2]**
A: [Answer]

**Q: [Common question 3]**
A: [Answer]

**Q: What if I get a question I can't answer?**
A: Say "That's a great question — I don't have the answer yet but I'll find out and get back to you by [timeframe]." Then contact [change team contact].

### Resources
- Full announcement text: [link]
- Detailed FAQ: [link]
- Change team contact: [name, email, Slack channel]
- Next manager briefing: [date]
```

### Template C: Stakeholder-Specific Impact Summary

**Use when:** Following up the broad announcement with specific details for a heavily impacted group.
**Sender:** Their direct VP or department leader (not generic "change team").
**Channel:** Department meeting or targeted email.

```
Subject: What [Change Name] Means for [Team/Department Name]

Hi [Team],

Following [Sponsor]'s announcement about [change], I want to share specifically 
what this means for our team.

**What's changing for us:**
- [Specific change 1 — describe in terms of their daily work]
- [Specific change 2]
- [Specific change 3]

**What's NOT changing:**
- [Stability point 1 — people need to know what stays the same]
- [Stability point 2]

**Timeline for our team:**
- [Date]: [Milestone]
- [Date]: [Milestone]
- [Date]: [Milestone]

**Training and support:**
- [What training they'll receive and when]
- [Who to contact for questions]
- [Where to find resources]

**What I need from you:**
- [Specific ask — attend training, try the new process, share feedback]

I'm holding [open office hours / Q&A session] on [date] for questions. 
You can also [other feedback channel].

[Name]
```

### Template E: Role-Specific "Day in the Life" Communication

**Use when:** Helping a specific role understand exactly how their daily work changes.
**Sender:** Their manager or a peer who has piloted the change.
**Channel:** Team meeting + written document.

```
## Your New Day-in-the-Life: [Role Name]

### Before the Change
[Describe their typical workflow in 5-7 steps using specific tools, processes, and interactions they'll recognize]

### After the Change  
[Describe the same workflow with the change applied — highlight what's different]

### What's Better
- [Concrete improvement 1 — time saved, friction removed, capability gained]
- [Concrete improvement 2]

### What Takes Getting Used To
- [Honest acknowledgment of what will feel harder or different at first]
- [How long the adjustment period typically takes]

### Tips from Pilot Users
- "[Specific tip from someone who tried it]" — [Name, Role]
- "[Specific tip]" — [Name, Role]

### Quick Reference
[Link to job aid, cheat sheet, or 1-page quick start guide]
```

**Use when:** A significant adoption milestone is reached.
**Sender:** Executive sponsor.
**Channel:** All-hands, email, or internal social.

## Channel Selection Matrix

Use this to match the right channel to the right message:

| Message Type | Best Channel | Backup Channel | Avoid |
|-------------|-------------|---------------|-------|
| Strategic announcement | Town hall / all-hands | Video message from sponsor | Email alone |
| Personal impact details | Manager 1:1 | Small group meeting | Mass email |
| Detailed process change | Workshop / training | Written guide + Q&A | Slide deck without discussion |
| Progress update | Email / Slack channel | Intranet post | Town hall (too heavyweight) |
| Success story | Internal social / newsletter | Team meeting shout-out | Formal announcement (too stiff) |
| Urgent issue / correction | Slack + manager cascade | Emergency email | Waiting for next scheduled update |
| FAQ / reference material | Intranet / wiki | Shared drive document | Email (gets lost) |
| Emotional / difficult news | In-person or live video | Manager 1:1 | Written communication alone |

## Cascade Briefing Structure

For large organizations, information must flow through layers. Design the cascade intentionally:

```
Day 0:   Sponsor → Senior Leadership (live briefing)
Day 1:   Senior Leaders → Their Direct Reports / VPs (live briefing)
Day 2:   VPs → People Managers (live briefing with talking points)
Day 3:   Broad announcement (all-hands or email)
Day 3-5: Managers → Their Teams (1:1 or team meeting)
Day 5-7: Open Q&A sessions / feedback channels
```

**Cascade rules:**
- Each layer must be briefed BEFORE the layer below them
- Each layer gets talking points, FAQ, and a clear "what to share and what not to share yet"
- Each layer has a feedback mechanism to escalate concerns upward
- Timing between layers should not exceed 48 hours — or the rumor mill fills the vacuum
