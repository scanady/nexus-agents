# Change Management Frameworks Reference

Deep-dive on industry-standard change management frameworks — when to use each, how to combine them, and how to adapt them to different change types.

---

## Framework Selection Guide

No single framework covers all aspects of change management. The best plans combine frameworks:

| Framework | Strength | Best For | Weakness |
|-----------|---------|---------|----------|
| **ADKAR** | Individual-level change tracking | Any change where individual adoption matters | Doesn't address organizational structure or culture |
| **Kotter 8-Step** | Building organizational momentum | Large-scale transformations needing enterprise buy-in | Linear model; can feel rigid; overemphasizes "burning platform" |
| **Prosci 3-Phase** | Structured project-level methodology | Technology implementations, process changes | Heavyweight for small changes; requires dedicated resources |
| **Bridges Transition Model** | Human psychological transition | Culture changes, restructuring, layoffs | Doesn't provide project management structure |
| **Lewin 3-Stage** | Simple conceptual clarity | Explaining change to non-practitioners | Too simplistic for complex initiatives |
| **McKinsey 7-S** | Organizational alignment diagnosis | Post-merger integration, strategic realignment | Diagnostic only — doesn't prescribe actions |

### Recommended Combinations

**Technology adoption:** ADKAR (individual tracking) + Prosci 3-Phase (project methodology)
**Culture transformation:** Kotter 8-Step (organizational momentum) + Bridges (psychological transition)
**Process redesign:** Prosci 3-Phase (structured methodology) + ADKAR (adoption tracking)
**Organizational restructuring:** Bridges (psychological) + McKinsey 7-S (organizational alignment)
**Merger/acquisition:** McKinsey 7-S (diagnostic) + Kotter (urgency and momentum) + Bridges (human transition)

## ADKAR Model (Prosci)

The ADKAR model tracks individual change readiness across five sequential building blocks. Each building block must be achieved before the next can succeed.

### The Five Building Blocks

**1. Awareness** — Understanding that a change is happening and why it is needed.
- Key question: Does the person understand the business reasons for the change?
- Built through: Communication, data sharing, case for change
- Common barrier: Inadequate or unclear communication; competing messages
- Assessment: Can they explain WHY the change is happening in their own words?

**2. Desire** — Personal motivation to participate in and support the change.
- Key question: Does the person want to change?
- Built through: WIIFM, manager coaching, peer influence, addressing fears
- Common barrier: Personal loss outweighs perceived gain; distrust of leadership
- Assessment: Are they engaging willingly or only under pressure?

**3. Knowledge** — Information on how to change (new skills, processes, behaviors).
- Key question: Does the person know what to do differently?
- Built through: Training, education, job aids, mentoring
- Common barrier: Training was awareness-level (what the tool does) not skill-level (how to use it in my job)
- Assessment: Can they describe the new process or demonstrate the new tool independently?

**4. Ability** — Demonstrated capability to implement the change in daily work.
- Key question: Can they actually do it?
- Built through: Practice, coaching, time, feedback, environmental support
- Common barrier: Knowing how is not the same as doing it; real-world complexity exceeds training scenarios
- Assessment: Are they performing the new behaviors correctly and consistently?

**5. Reinforcement** — Mechanisms that sustain the change and prevent regression.
- Key question: Will they keep doing it?
- Built through: Recognition, measurement, accountability, continuous improvement
- Common barrier: Old habits under pressure; no consequences for reverting; no recognition for adopting
- Assessment: Are new behaviors persisting 90+ days after implementation?

### Using ADKAR for Diagnosis

The power of ADKAR is diagnosis: identify the first barrier point for each stakeholder group and focus your intervention there.

**Example diagnosis:**
- If a group has high Awareness but low Desire → communication isn't the problem; WIIFM and trust are
- If a group has high Desire but low Knowledge → they want to change but training hasn't been adequate
- If a group has high Knowledge but low Ability → training was theoretical; they need hands-on practice

**Rule:** Always address the earliest barrier first. Building Knowledge when Desire is absent wastes everyone's time.

## Kotter's 8-Step Model

John Kotter's model focuses on building organizational momentum for large-scale change. It's sequential but iterative — you may need to return to earlier steps.

### The 8 Steps

**Phase 1: Create the Climate for Change**

**Step 1: Create a Sense of Urgency**
- Build the case for why the status quo is unacceptable
- Use data: market trends, competitive threats, financial projections, customer feedback
- The urgency must be real, not manufactured — people detect false urgency
- Warning: "Burning platform" messaging has a shelf life; urgency alone doesn't sustain change

**Step 2: Build a Guiding Coalition**
- Assemble a group with enough power, credibility, and diversity to lead the change
- Must include: sponsor (authority), respected practitioners (credibility), diverse voices (perspective)
- The coalition must be visibly aligned — one dissenting senior leader undermines everything
- Size: 3-7 people for most organizations; larger for enterprise-scale transformations

**Step 3: Form a Strategic Vision and Initiatives**
- Create a clear, compelling picture of the future state
- The vision must be: specific enough to guide decisions, compelling enough to motivate, and simple enough to communicate in 5 minutes
- Back the vision with 3-5 strategic initiatives that make it real

**Phase 2: Engage and Enable the Organization**

**Step 4: Enlist a Volunteer Army**
- Broad-based buy-in from people across the organization
- Not forced compliance — genuine engagement through involvement and influence
- Change champions, early adopters, pilot groups, feedback panels
- Leverage social networks: people trust peers more than executives

**Step 5: Enable Action by Removing Barriers**
- Identify and remove structural, process, and cultural barriers to adoption
- Common barriers: incompatible systems, conflicting incentives, skills gaps, middle manager resistance
- Empower people to act: remove approval bottlenecks, provide resources, update policies

**Step 6: Generate Short-Term Wins**
- Plan for and create visible, meaningful wins early in the initiative
- Wins must be: visible (people can see them), unambiguous (clearly linked to the change), and meaningful (not trivial)
- Short-term wins build momentum and silence skeptics
- Timing: first visible win within 30-60 days if possible

**Phase 3: Implement and Sustain the Change**

**Step 7: Sustain Acceleration**
- Use credibility from early wins to drive deeper change
- Don't declare victory too early — premature celebration is the #1 killer of change initiatives
- Continue removing barriers, adjusting the plan, and maintaining urgency

**Step 8: Institute Change**
- Anchor new behaviors in organizational culture
- Embed in: performance management, hiring criteria, onboarding, recognition programs
- Articulate the connection between new behaviors and organizational success
- Institutional memory: document what changed and why so it persists beyond the change team

## Prosci 3-Phase Process

Prosci provides a structured, project-level methodology for managing change alongside the technical implementation.

### Phase 1: Preparing for Change

**Activities:**
- Define the change (scope, objectives, impacted groups)
- Assess change readiness (organizational and individual)
- Develop the change management strategy
- Assemble the change team
- Define success metrics

**Key deliverables:**
- Change characteristics assessment
- Organizational readiness assessment
- Sponsorship assessment
- Change management strategy document

### Phase 2: Managing Change

**Activities:**
- Develop and execute communication plan
- Develop and execute sponsorship roadmap
- Develop and execute coaching plan (for managers)
- Develop and execute training plan
- Develop and execute resistance management plan

**Key deliverables:**
- Communication plan with messages, channels, and timeline
- Sponsor roadmap with visible actions
- Manager coaching plan
- Training delivery plan
- Resistance management plan

### Phase 3: Reinforcing Change

**Activities:**
- Collect and analyze feedback
- Diagnose gaps and manage resistance
- Implement corrective actions
- Celebrate successes
- Transfer ownership from change team to business

**Key deliverables:**
- Adoption and usage metrics
- Compliance assessment
- Corrective action plans
- Lessons learned documentation
- Sustainment plan

## Bridges Transition Model

William Bridges distinguishes between **change** (external, situational) and **transition** (internal, psychological). Change is what happens to people; transition is what happens within them.

### The Three Phases of Transition

**1. Ending, Losing, Letting Go**
- People must first let go of the old way before they can embrace the new
- This phase involves grief, loss, and resistance — even when the change is positive
- What people lose: competence (they knew how to do the old way), relationships (teams may change), identity (their role or expertise may shift), control (familiar routines are disrupted)
- **Leader actions:** Acknowledge the loss. Name what's ending. Let people express frustration. Don't rush past this phase.

**2. The Neutral Zone**
- The in-between state where the old way is gone but the new way isn't yet established
- Characterized by: confusion, anxiety, low productivity, creativity, innovation
- This is the most dangerous phase — people are uncertain, prone to reverting, and vulnerable to rumors
- **Leader actions:** Provide structure and short-term goals. Increase communication frequency. Normalize the discomfort. Create quick wins. Set up temporary support systems.

**3. The New Beginning**
- People emotionally commit to the new way and experience renewed energy and identity
- Characterized by: sense of purpose, new competence, new relationships, new identity
- New beginnings can't be forced — they happen when people are ready
- **Leader actions:** Reinforce the new identity. Celebrate progress. Connect new behaviors to purpose. Recognize early adopters. Be patient with late adopters.

### Applying Bridges in Practice

The Bridges model is most valuable for:
- Planning the **emotional** arc of a change initiative alongside the operational timeline
- Understanding why people resist even "obviously good" changes
- Designing communication that meets people where they are psychologically
- Giving leaders a framework for empathetic, not just efficient, change leadership

## Lewin's 3-Stage Model

Kurt Lewin's model is the simplest change framework. Useful for explaining the basic mechanics of change but insufficient on its own for complex initiatives.

### The 3 Stages

**1. Unfreeze** — Create readiness for change
- Challenge the status quo; build awareness of the need
- Equivalent to: ADKAR Awareness/Desire, Kotter Steps 1-3, Bridges Ending

**2. Change** — Implement the new state
- Execute the transition; provide training and support
- Equivalent to: ADKAR Knowledge/Ability, Kotter Steps 4-6, Bridges Neutral Zone

**3. Refreeze** — Stabilize the new state
- Embed new behaviors; reinforce and sustain
- Equivalent to: ADKAR Reinforcement, Kotter Steps 7-8, Bridges New Beginning

### Force Field Analysis (Lewin)

A useful diagnostic tool from Lewin: map the forces driving and restraining the change.

```
DRIVING FORCES → ← RESTRAINING FORCES
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━
Market pressure    →  ←  Change fatigue
Customer demand    →  ←  Skill gaps
Cost reduction     →  ←  Distrust of leadership
Regulatory mandate →  ←  Competing priorities
Technology gains   →  ←  Cultural attachment
```

**Strategy:** Don't just increase driving forces (more urgency, more pressure). Also reduce restraining forces (address fatigue, close skill gaps, rebuild trust). Reducing restraint is often more effective and less disruptive than increasing pressure.

## McKinsey 7-S Framework

A diagnostic framework for assessing organizational alignment. Useful before and during change to identify misalignment.

### The 7 Elements

| Element | Category | Description |
|---------|----------|-------------|
| **Strategy** | Hard | The organization's plan for achieving competitive advantage |
| **Structure** | Hard | How the organization is organized (hierarchy, reporting, teams) |
| **Systems** | Hard | Processes, tools, and workflows that support daily operations |
| **Shared Values** | Soft | Core beliefs and attitudes that shape the culture |
| **Style** | Soft | Leadership approach and organizational management style |
| **Staff** | Soft | People, their capabilities, and how they are developed |
| **Skills** | Soft | Distinctive capabilities of the organization and its people |

### Using 7-S for Change

When planning a change, assess which elements are affected and which are aligned:

1. Map the current state of all 7 elements
2. Map the desired future state of all 7 elements
3. Identify gaps between current and desired state
4. Identify misalignment between elements (e.g., new strategy but old structure; new systems but old skills)
5. Plan interventions for each misaligned element

**Key insight:** Changing one element without adjusting the others creates tension and undermines adoption. A new technology (Systems) without new training (Skills) and updated processes (Structure) will fail.
